Power Rule for Differentiation
The derivative of xⁿ is n·xⁿ⁻¹ for any positive integer n.
Definition of derivative
📈 A rocket scientist needs to calculate the velocity of a spacecraft at every instant. Position s(t) = t³. By definition, velocity = derivative = limit of (s(t+h)−s(t))/h as h→0.
Expand using binomial theorem
📐 Expand (x+h)ⁿ using the binomial theorem. The terms are: xⁿ + n·xⁿ⁻¹·h + n(n−1)/2!·xⁿ⁻²·h² + ... + hⁿ.
Cancel xⁿ terms
🧮 Subtract xⁿ and divide by h. The xⁿ cancels, and every remaining term contains h. Factor h: the numerator becomes h[n·xⁿ⁻¹ + ... + hⁿ⁻¹]. Cancel h with denominator.
Take limit as h→0
✅ As h→0, all terms with h vanish, leaving only n·xⁿ⁻¹. For the rocket: if s(t) = t³, velocity v(t) = 3t². At t=5 seconds: v = 3×25 = 75 m/s. The power rule makes differentiation instant!

Derivative of Sine Function
The derivative of sin x is cos x.
🎵 An audio engineer analyzing sound waves needs to know how the amplitude changes. A sine wave sin(440t) represents musical note A440. The rate of change gives the velocity of the speaker cone.
Apply sum identity
📐 Use the identity sin(A+B) = sin A cos B + cos A sin B: sin(x+h) = sin x cos h + cos x sin h.
Group and rearrange
🧮 Factor: = lim[sin x(cos h − 1)/h + cos x(sin h)/h]. Two special limits emerge!
Apply known limits
✅ The two fundamental limits: (1) lim(cos h − 1)/h = 0 (the graph of cos is flat at h=0), (2) lim(sin h)/h = 1 (the small-angle approximation). Result: d/dx sin x = sin x·0 + cos x·1 = cos x. Similarly, d/dx cos x = −sin x.

Derivatives of Inverse Trigonometric Functions
Derivatives of inverse trig functions using implicit differentiation.
Let y = sin⁻¹ x ⇒ x = sin y
🎯 A ballistics expert measures the ratio of vertical to horizontal velocity (x) and needs the angle. If x = 0.5, then y = sin⁻¹(0.5) = 30°. Implicit differentiation finds how the angle changes with the ratio.
Differentiate implicitly
📐 Using implicit differentiation: d/dx(x) = d/dx(sin y) → 1 = cos y · dy/dx → dy/dx = 1/cos y.
Substitute cos y = √(1−sin²y)
🧮 Using the identity cos²y + sin²y = 1: cos y = √(1−sin²y) = √(1−x²). Therefore d/dx sin⁻¹ x = 1/√(1−x²).
Same process for tan⁻¹ x
✅ For tan⁻¹: differentiate x = tan y → 1 = sec²y·dy/dx. Since sec²y = 1 + tan²y = 1 + x², we get d/dx tan⁻¹ x = 1/(1+x²). These formulas are the key to integrating rational functions!

The special case n = −1
📐 The power rule fails when n = −1 (would give division by zero). But d/dx[ln x] = 1/x, so ∫ 1/x dx = ln|x| + C. This is the bridge between algebraic and logarithmic functions.
Integral of exponential aˣ
🧮 Differentiate aˣ/ln a: the derivative is aˣ·ln a / ln a = aˣ. Therefore ∫ aˣ dx = aˣ/ln a + C. For the natural exponential: ∫ eˣ dx = eˣ + C (since ln e = 1).
Linearity of integration
✅ Integration is linear: the integral of a sum is the sum of integrals, and constants factor out. This is why ∫ (3x² + 2x + 1) dx = 3·x³/3 + 2·x²/2 + x + C = x³ + x² + x + C.

Concurrency of Altitudes in a Triangle
The three altitudes of a triangle always intersect at a single point (the orthocenter).
Define vertices and slopes
🏔️ A surveyor is mapping a triangular plot of land. To find the orthocenter (where all lines of elevation meet), they need to prove the three altitudes intersect at one point.
Slope of BC → altitude AD
📐 The slope of BC is (y₃−y₂)/(x₃−x₂). Altitude AD ∦ BC, so its slope is the negative reciprocal: m_AD = −(x₃−x₂)/(y₃−y₂).
Equation of altitude AD
🧮 Using point-slope form with point A(x₁, y₁): y − y₁ = m_AD(x − x₁). Rearranging: (x₃−x₂)x + (y₃−y₂)y − x₁(x₃−x₂) − y₁(y₃−y₂) = 0.
Determinant of coefficients = 0
✅ Write similar equations for BE and CF. Place their coefficients in a determinant. Adding R₂ + R₃ to R₁ makes the first row zero (since the sums of coefficients from each row cancel). A zero row means the determinant = 0, proving the lines are concurrent at the orthocenter!
