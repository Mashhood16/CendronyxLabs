Here are the detailed concepts for the oxygen and carbon cycles, followed by a highly detailed list of all 34 formal practical activities in your textbook.

### **The Oxygen and Carbon Cycles**
*   **The Oxygen Cycle:** Earth's atmosphere contains 21% oxygen. This oxygen is largely produced by plants during **photosynthesis**, where they consume carbon dioxide and release oxygen as a byproduct. Both animals and plants then continuously consume this oxygen during **respiration** to extract energy from their food. 
*   **The Carbon Cycle:** Carbon dioxide (CO₂) makes up 0.04% of the atmosphere. Plants absorb CO₂ to prepare organic food compounds via photosynthesis. Animals gain this carbon when they feed on plants or other animals. The respiration process of both plants and animals then releases CO₂ back into the environment. 
*   **Fossil Fuels & Combustion:** Dead plants and animals buried under the earth's crust are converted into fossil fuels like coal, oil, and gas over time. When humans burn these fuels in factories and automobiles (a combustion reaction), it rapidly adds carbon dioxide, heat, and light back into the atmosphere. 
*   **The Chemical Equation:** These cycles run in opposite, complementary directions to sustain life, represented by the reversible equation: $6CO_2 + 6H_2O \rightleftharpoons C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2$.
*   **Related Field Activity:** To observe how excess carbon impacts temperatures, you can visit a local greenhouse, measure the temperature inside versus outside, and observe how the glass/plastic structure traps heat just like greenhouse gases do in our atmosphere.

### **Detailed List of Every Practical Activity**

**Unit 1: Ecology**
*   **Activity 1.1 Greenhouse effect:** Place two thermometers in the sun, putting one of them inside a closed glass jar. After 20 minutes, read the temperatures to observe that the closed jar is hotter, demonstrating how trapped heat mimics the atmospheric greenhouse effect.

**Unit 2: Human Nervous System**
*   **Activity 2.1 Animal's response to stimulus:** Cover one half of a petri dish with black paper to make it dark. Place five termites in the bright side and five in the dark side, then cover with a clear lid. After half an hour, observe their distribution to see how they physically respond to the light stimulus.
*   **Activity 2.2 The knee jerk reflex:** Sit and cross your legs so the shin of your upper leg swings freely. Give a sharp tap just below the kneecap (patellar tendon) with the edge of your hand. Your thigh muscle (quadriceps) will have an automatic, involuntary kicking reaction.
*   **Activity 2.3 Reflex action time:** Have Student B drop a ruler while Student A tries to catch it as quickly as possible. Student C records the time using a stopwatch. Record the catch time and distance for at least ten students to find the shortest reaction time and class average.

**Unit 3: Variations, Heredity & Cell Division**
*   **Activity 3.1 DNA extraction:** Mash a 1-inch piece of soft fruit (banana or strawberry) with 30 ml of water and a pinch of table salt. Filter the mixture through a cloth into a jar. Add two drops of dish soap, swirl gently without making bubbles, and slowly pour 30 ml of chilled isopropyl alcohol down the side of the jar. White, fluffy strands of DNA will precipitate at the boundary.
*   **Activity 3.2 Variations in humans:** Develop a questionnaire and collect data from at least 20 people on traits like blood group, tongue rolling, skin color, ear lobes (free/attached), and height. Use the data to separate the traits into continuous and discontinuous variations.
*   **Activity 3.3 Crossing over:** Create two identical paper chromosomes and color them differently. Exchange their segments using a cutter to physically model the process of genetic crossing over and recombination.

**Unit 4: Biotechnology**
*   **Activity 4.1 Making of yogurt:** Boil two cups of milk for half an hour to sterilize it, then let it cool to room temperature. Mix in a spoon of existing yogurt. Cover the pot with a towel and incubate it in a dark, warm place for 4 to 5 hours until the bacteria successfully ferments the milk into yogurt.

**Unit 5: The Periodic Table of Elements**
*   **Activity 5.1 Elements in periodic table:** Use the periodic table to count and record the number of elements found in the first, second, third, fourth, fifth, and sixth periods.
*   **Activity 5.2 Electronic configuration:** Draw the electronic structures for Group IA elements (Lithium, Sodium, Potassium) and Group IIA elements (Beryllium, Magnesium, Calcium). Observe that Group IA elements contain 1 electron in their outermost shell, while Group IIA elements contain 2.
*   **Activity 5.3 Malleability:** Beat thick pieces of iron, copper, aluminum, coke (carbon), and sulphur with a hammer. The metals will flatten and become thinner (malleable), while the non-metals will break into smaller pieces.
*   **Activity 5.4 Flexibility:** Bend metal wires (copper, iron, aluminum) into different shapes and attempt to bend a graphite carbon rod. Metals are flexible and will not break, while non-metals will break easily.
*   **Activity 5.5 Density:** Place small bars or sheets of metals (copper, iron, aluminum) and non-metals on the surface of water in a trough. Observe which ones sink and which float to determine their relative densities.
*   **Activity 5.6 Thermal conductivity:** Place a metal spoon and a wooden spoon in a bowl of hot soup. The metal spoon will conduct heat and become hot quickly, while the wood will not.
*   **Activity 5.7 Sonorous nature:** Strike an aluminum plate, a copper plate, and a wooden or plastic plate with a hammer. Only the metals will produce a deep or ringing (sonorous) sound.

**Unit 6: Chemical Reactions and Bonding**
*   **Activity 6.1 Identifying signs of a chemical reaction:** 
    *   Mix iodine and starch in a beaker to observe a color change from brown to blue. 
    *   Mix copper sulphate and sodium hydroxide to observe the formation of a blue solid precipitate. 
    *   Mix 25 cm³ of vinegar and half a spoon of baking soda to observe effervescence (gas emission) and a cold temperature change.
*   **Activity 6.2 Law of conservation of mass:** Place 50 cm³ of sodium sulphate solution in a conical flask and 20 cm³ of calcium chloride in a test tube inside the flask. Weigh the entire apparatus. Mix them to form a white precipitate (calcium sulphate) and weigh the apparatus again to prove the total mass remains perfectly constant before and after the reaction.
*   **Activity 6.3 Endothermic reactions:** Place 50 cm³ of vinegar in a beaker and record its temperature. Add half a spoon of baking soda and shake. A suspended thermometer will record a decrease in temperature, demonstrating that the reaction absorbs energy.
*   **Activity 6.4 Exothermic reactions:** Place 50 cm³ of water in a beaker, record the temperature, and add one spoon of calcium chloride and magnesium sulphate. Stir with a glass rod. The thermometer will show a rise in temperature, indicating the reaction releases energy.

**Unit 7: Acids, Bases and Salts**
*   **Activity 7.1 Effects of acids on Litmus paper:** Dip blue and red litmus paper into lemon juice, vinegar, and the cut surface of an apple. In all cases, the blue litmus paper will turn red, indicating the presence of an acid.
*   **Activity 7.2 Effects of alkalies on Litmus paper:** Dissolve two pellets of sodium hydroxide in 10 cm³ of water. Dip blue and red litmus paper into the solution; the red paper will turn blue, indicating it is an alkali.
*   **Activity 7.3 Neutralization reaction:** *(Teacher demonstration only due to corrosive NaOH)*. Transfer 25 cm³ of sodium hydroxide into a flask and add phenolphthalein indicator, turning it pink. Add hydrochloric acid from a burette drop by drop until the pink color entirely disappears, marking the exact end point of the neutralization reaction.
*   **Activity 7.4 Determining pH:** Dip pH paper into strong acids (HCl, H₂SO₄), strong alkalies (NaOH, KOH), sugar solutions, and distilled water. Compare the color developed on the pH paper to determine the exact numerical pH value.

**Unit 8: Force and Pressure**
*   **Activity 8.1 Pushing a nail into wood:** Try to push a nail into a wooden plank by its flat head, and then by its pointed end. The pointed end penetrates easily because the force is concentrated over a smaller area, creating much higher pressure.
*   **Activity 8.2 Hydraulic elevator:** Connect a 10 ml and a 20 ml syringe with a connecting tube and fill it with water or oil. Glue a wall plate to the 20 ml syringe to act as an elevator platform and mount it to a stand. Place a toy car on the platform and push the smaller syringe to lift it, demonstrating how fluid pressure multiplies force.
*   **Activity 8.3 Building a water pressure rocket:** Decorate an empty 2-liter bottle with a cardboard cone and 4 fins. Drill a hole in a cork and push a bicycle pump needle adaptor through it. Fill the bottle one-quarter full of water and tightly insert the cork. Go outside and pump air in until the built-up pressure forcefully expels the water downward, propelling the rocket high into the sky.

**Unit 9: Reflection and Refraction of Light**
*   **Activity 9.1 Tracing mirror reflections:** Draw an obstacle maze with an entrance and exit on a piece of chart paper. Use small plane mirror strips, binder clips, and a laser light source to bounce a light beam around the obstacles and successfully navigate the maze.
*   **Activity 9.3 Making a periscope:** Cut windows and 45-degree angled slits into two empty juice cartons. Insert mirrors into the slits with their shiny sides facing the windows. Join the two cartons together to construct a functional periscope.
*   **Activity 9.4 The bending of pencil:** Fill identical glasses with different amounts of water or fluid and place a pencil in each. Observe refraction, as the pencil appears bent or broken where it enters the liquid because light travels slower in water than air.
*   **Activity 9.5 Shaving mirror:** Hold a concave make-up or shaving mirror close to your face and slowly back away across the room. Your image will start upright and magnified, disappear at the focal point, and then re-appear upside down as you move further away.
*   **Activity 9.6 Car rearview mirror:** Sit in the driver's seat of a car and compare an object seen in the flat inside back-view mirror with the same object seen in the passenger-side convex mirror. The convex mirror provides a wider field of view by making the image smaller.

**Unit 10: Electricity and Magnetism**
*   **Activity 10.1 Making an electromagnet:** Remove the insulation from the ends of a wire and tightly wind it around an iron nail. Connect the bare ends to a battery cell to magnetize the nail and test its strength by picking up paper clips. Move a compass near the nail to map the magnetic field, and reverse the battery connections to see the compass needle change direction.

**Unit 11: Technology in Everyday Life**
*   **Activity 11.1 Make your own toothpaste:** Mix 2 teaspoons of glycerin, 3 teaspoons of baking soda, a pinch of salt, and 5 drops of peppermint oil in a mortar or ceramic container to form a paste.
*   **Activity 11.2 Make your own soap:** Wearing disposable gloves and goggles, dissolve 15g of pure lye in 30g of distilled water. Heat 56g coconut oil and 28g olive oil in a pan to 50°C. Pour the lye solution into the oil and blend until a thick paste forms. Pour into molds and leave to cure for two days.
*   **Activity 11.3 Plastic from milk:** Warm 1 cup of milk in a pan and stir in 4 cups of vinegar, which will separate the milk into curds and whey. Strain out the liquid whey and use paper towels to remove excess moisture. Knead the remaining curds to form a moldable casein milk plastic.
*   **Activity 11.4 Make your own detergent:** Combine 2 cups washing soda, 2 cups baking soda, 2 cups borax, and a few drops of essential oil in a container. Secure the lid and shake thoroughly to mix.
*   **Activity 11.5 & 11.6 Solar Cookers:** Follow safety instructions for cooking with focused solar reflections (wearing protective glasses and using a dark pot). Construct a parabolic concave mirror solar cooker using thin plywood petals bound by cable ties and lined with adhesive aluminum tape.
*   **Activity 11.7 Designing a wind turbine:** Attach cardboard or Styrofoam cup blades to a small DC toy motor using a cork and a common pin. Mount the motor on a stand and place it near a pedestal fan. Connect the wires to a voltmeter to measure the electrical voltage produced by the wind.
*   **Activity 11.8 Chemical reaction and engineering in action:** Build a car chassis from a plastic bottle, using bamboo skewers as axles and pony beads glued inside plastic caps as wheels. Pour vinegar into the bottle. Roll 1 teaspoon of baking soda inside a piece of tissue paper, insert it into the bottle, and quickly seal it with a cap that has a fine melted hole in the center. Shake the bottle; the escaping carbon dioxide gas will propel the car forward.
*   **Activity 11.9 Design a windsock:** Create and install a windsock at a high point in your school to visualize the direction of the wind.

**Unit 12: Our Universe**
*   **Activity 12.1 Sky map mobile app:** Download a smartphone stargazing app (like SkyView or Sky Map) . Point your phone at the night sky to identify constellations and use the search function to locate Alpha Centauri near the Southern Cross .
